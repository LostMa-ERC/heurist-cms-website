When a text is composed in verse, the rhyme pattern.

Term Description Reference
alliteration Rhyme is allowed between words that either start with the same consonant sound or with the same vowel, such as “With floures fele, fair under fete” in Middle English. S. Davis. “Guillaume de Machaut, Chaucer's "Book of the Duchess", and the Chaucer Tradition”. In: The Chaucer Review 36.4 (2002), pp. 391-405. issn: 00092002, 15284204. url: http://www.jstor.org/stable/25096182 (visited on 06/20/2024), p. 396.
assonance Rhyme is allowed between words that have a repeated vowel sound, such as “a” in “Vio puertas abiertas e uços sin cañados / alcandaras vazias sin pielles e sin mantos” in Castilian. J. Gornall. “Assonance in the Hispanic "Romance": Precept and Practice”. In: The Modern Language Review 90.2 (1995), pp. 363-369. issn: 00267937, 22224319. url: http://www.jstor.org/stable/3734546 (visited on 06/20/2024), p. 364.
end-rhyme Rhyme is allowed between words that have an ending that sounds the same, such as “ihesu guz son ihesu goþe / bløt mit hiærta mæþ þino bloþe” in Old Norse. W. Layher. “The Big Splash: End-Rhyme and Innovation in Medieval Scandinavian Poetics”. In: Scandinavian Studies 80.4 (2008), pp. 407-436. issn: 00365637. url: http://www.jstor.org/stable/40920821 (visited on 06/20/2024), p. 423.
generic “[R]hyme is allowed between any one member of a phonetic group and is itself or any other member of the same group,” such as 'b,' 'g,' 'd' in Old Irish." M. McKie. “The Origins and Early Development of Rhyme in English Verse”. In: The Modern Language Review 92.4 (1997), pp. 817-831. issn: 00267937, 22224319. url: http://www.jstor.org/stable/3734202 (visited on 06/20/2024), p. 822.
